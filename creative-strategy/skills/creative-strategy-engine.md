# ⚙️ Creative Strategy Engine

*A systematic framework for generating direct response ad variations*
*@alyshafrommotion*

---

This is the complete process from understanding your product and customer all the way down to executing the actual ad. It's a full map you can navigate to get to one really intentional ad, or hundreds of ads that cover a full funnel and are mapped strategically. It applies to any brand, any product, any campaign.

---

## Step 1: Understand the Product

**What are the features? What does the competitive landscape look like?**

Before you can sell anything, you need to know exactly what you're selling — and what you're selling against.

### Your product

- What does it do?
- What is it made of or what does it contain?
- How does it work?
- What are its functional specifications?
- What does it include?

### Product variations

Map all variations of the product — different formats, sizes, flavors, tiers, or configurations. Variations matter because different versions may speak to different personas, solve different versions of the same problem, or create different offer framings.

### Competitive landscape

Map the same features for your key competitors. The goal is to understand what you have that they don't, what they have that you don't, and how every meaningful product attribute compares side by side.

- What features or attributes do competitors have that you don't?
- What do you have that competitors don't?
- Where do you win on the comparison? Where do you lose?



---

## Step 2: Understand the Customer

Break your customers into segments. A customer segment is a group with shared demographics, life context, and a relationship to the category. You will have multiple segments. Each one can map to multiple pains or desires in Step 3.

### Customer segments

For each segment, understand:

- Who are they? Demographics: age, gender, income, location, life stage
- Psychographics: values, beliefs, lifestyle, aspirations
- What language do they use to describe their problem or desire?
- What is their relationship to this category — new, experienced, or burned by something that didn't work?
- What goals do they have related to this product or category?
- What platforms do they spend time on?
- What content are they consuming on those platforms?
- Who are their influencers?
---

## Step 3: Map pain or desire to a customer segment — and define the micropersona

**Choose a customer segment and a specific pain or desire. The intersection of the two is where the micropersona is defined.**

Any product can be found or desired in two ways: through pain (a problem they have) or through desire (something they want). When you choose your intersection, you are choosing which one you are building this message around. Both can be valid for the same product — this step is about choosing one for this specific message.

This step has two parts: map and go deep.

### The mapping

For each pain or desire, identify which personas connect to it. Then choose one intersection to develop. That intersection is your working unit for the rest of the framework.

A persona maps to multiple pains and desires. Multiple personas map to the same pain or desire. The grid of intersections is the full map of your creative opportunities.

### The micropersona

**What it is:** A fully realized human being at this specific intersection — not just who they are, but how they live with this specific pain or desire. This is the output of placing a persona inside the context of a pain or desire and going deep on their experience of it. This is the level you write creative from.

- What is their pain, or what do they desire?
- How do they experience that problem in their daily life — or how do they envision achieving that desire?
- What is their emotional and physical state around it?
- What is their history with this problem or desire?
- What have they tried?
- What have they accepted or normalized?
- What experiences or influences shaped how they relate to this today?
- What are they afraid of?
- What do they wish they could change?

---

## Step 4: Define the Offer

**You have the micropersona. Now define what you are offering them.**

You know who you are speaking to, what their specific pain or desire is, and how they live with it. This is where that customer knowledge meets your product knowledge from Step 1. Work through these four questions in sequence.

| Who are you speaking to? | What is their pain or desire? | Which features solve for this? | How do you convey that as an offering? |
|--------------------------|-------------------------------|-------------------------------|----------------------------------------|
| The specific micropersona | In their own language — the felt experience, not the category label | From Step 1 — the ones directly relevant to this person's specific problem or desire | Translate those features into the benefit that matters to her in the context of her daily life |

### Selling vs. offering

There is a meaningful distinction between selling a product and offering something.

**Selling** says: "Here, buy this thing."
**Offering** says: "Here is a solution to your problem" — or — "Here is a means to who you want to be."

The offer statement is not a description of the product. It is a description of what the product means for this person.

**❌ Selling:** "An electrolyte supplement with 150mg of sodium"
**✓ Offering:** "The thing that stops the headache before it becomes 48 hours of your life gone"

**❌ Selling:** "A tween bra with adjustable straps"
**✓ Offering:** "A bra that grows with your daughter so you don't have to keep buying new ones"

A strong offering names her specific outcome, sounds like something she would say she needs, and sets up the messaging angle directly.

## Step 5: Choose your funnel stage

**Who are you reaching, and what do you need to accomplish?**

The funnel stage is a strategic decision made before any creative work begins. It determines which group of people you are trying to reach and what your content needs to do for them. Every other creative choice — the lens, the hook, the format — serves this goal.

### The 5 awareness stages

**Unaware**
Not in market. They don't know they have the problem yet — or haven't felt it enough to act on it.
Goal: Reveal the problem or spark the desire.
Approach: Education, observation, pattern recognition.
Energy: *"Hm, wait — is that what's happening to me?"*

---

**Problem-Aware**
In market, but not yet looking at solutions. They know they have the problem — they just haven't started looking for a fix.
Goal: Agitate the pain, validate their experience.
Approach: Empathy, pain callouts, "tired of...?" framing.
Energy: *"Yes, exactly. This is my life."*

---

**Solution-Aware**
In market and actively looking. They're exploring different categories or approaches to solve the problem.
Goal: Position your category or approach as the right one.
Approach: Comparison, "there's a better way," education.
Energy: *"I didn't know that was an option."*

---

**Product-Aware**
In market and aware of your product. They're evaluating it against other options — this is the consideration stage.
Goal: Overcome objections, prove why this product over the alternatives.
Approach: Reviews, proof, guarantees, head-to-head comparisons.
Energy: *"Okay, this one might actually be different."*

---

**Most-Aware**
Ready to purchase. They've made their decision — they just need a reason to act now.
Goal: Close.
Approach: Urgency, direct offer, guarantee, clear CTA.
Energy: *"Fine. I'm in. Tell me how."*

---

### Funnel strategy for paid social

**Where to start:** Product-Aware. These are the people actively evaluating options — the highest-value, most convertible audience that isn't already decided. Add a small number of Most-Aware ads for specific, intentional purposes only: a sale event, a limited-time offer, clearing a specific SKU fast. Most-Aware ads speak to people who were already going to buy — they accelerate a decision, but they don't create one. Over-indexing here means you're spending money on people you would have won anyway.

**How to scale:** Once Product-Aware is working, expand upward — into Solution-Aware, then Problem-Aware, then Unaware. Upper-funnel content finds new customers who were never in your market, pulls people who were close into the funnel, and moves existing audiences through it faster. This is how an account grows. The upper funnel is not a nice-to-have. It is where new customers come from.

**What to avoid:** Never let Most-Aware dominate your creative mix outside of intentional sale periods. It's the smallest possible lever — you're only reaching the people who were already going to convert. An account that over-indexes here will plateau.

## Step 6: Choose your messaging lens

**Now that you know the stage, how will you communicate the offer?**

The stage gives you the strategic goal. The lens is the creative approach you take to convey the offer to this specific person at this point in their journey. The same offer can be communicated through any of these lenses — the stage you chose in Step 6 will naturally point you toward the ones that fit.

### The lenses

**Desired Outcome 💖**
What's the best-case version of their life if this problem is solved? Lead with where they want to be. The transformation, not the problem.

**Pain Agitation 😣**
Don't rush to the solution. Sit inside the pain with them. Describe it in detail — what it feels like, what it costs them, how it shows up in their daily life. When someone reads your hook and thinks "this person gets exactly what I'm going through," they stop. Most powerful for Problem-Aware audiences who know the pain but haven't felt urgency to fix it.

**Use Case 💡**
Show them a specific, recognizable moment from their real life where this product fits naturally. Not a demo — a scene. The more specific the situation, the harder it is to scroll past.

**Consequences ⚠️**
What gets worse if they don't solve this? Lead with the cost of inaction. The negative trajectory they're already on makes the solution feel urgent rather than optional.

**Acceptance 🤷**
What frustration or limitation have they normalized because they don't believe it can be solved — or have tried and given up on? Position the product as proof they don't have to accept this. Change is possible.

**Education 🎓**
What don't they know about the problem, the solution, or the product that you can teach them? What gap can you fill to surface the problem, make it feel more urgent, or shift their perception of the solution? This isn't about busting a myth — it's about teaching something new.

**Misconceptions 😤**
What common misconception is holding the customer back? What belief needs to be rebutted for them to take a next step? This isn't about educating — it's about removing a blocker between them and change.

**Comparison 😱**
What have they already tried that didn't work? How does this product compare? Position the product as the solution through Us vs. Them or Before and After framing.

**Features/Benefits ✅**
Which specific aspect of the product, when explained properly, makes the customer realize this is the right solution? Lead with the feature that most directly addresses their pain, then name the benefit it delivers.

**Objections 👎**
What specific doubt or hesitation would stop them from buying right now? Address it head-on. Turning skepticism into the hook itself disarms resistance before it forms.

**Identity 🧍**
What insecurity do they carry about who they are today as it relates to this category? Who do they look at and think "I want to be like that"? Position the product as the path to becoming that person.

---

The lens you choose becomes the hook concept. The hook is the opening expression of the offer, through this lens, for this person, at this stage.

### Messaging angle template

```
STAGE: [Funnel stage]
PERSONA: [Persona name and brief description]
PAIN/DESIRE: [The pain or desire]
LENS: [Which lens you are using]

HOOK CONCEPT: "[Core angle statement]"

Context:
- How they experience this in their life:
- What they really want as an outcome:
- Features that matter most to them:
- Objections they might have:
```

## Step 7: Execute in multiple formats

**What visual format brings this to life?**

Executing one messaging angle across multiple formats gives you more chances to connect and contributes to creative diversity. Different people stop for different things.

Format is the container for the message. The right container depends on what the message needs to do — and what the message needs to do depends on the awareness stage you are speaking to. Format choice is not arbitrary. A format that works well for one stage may be a poor fit for another. An Us vs. Them format is a natural container for Product-Aware content, where the customer is evaluating options. It makes no sense for Unaware, where the customer doesn't yet know they have a problem — and wouldn't understand what they're being compared against.

### Format categories by purpose

**Formats that educate and reveal** — best for Unaware, Problem-Aware
- Storytelling
- Before/After
- Brain Scan View
- ASMR

**Formats that compare and demonstrate** — best for Solution-Aware, Product-Aware
- Talking Head
- Greenscreen Library
- Vs./Them
- How To
- Feature/Benefit Portrait

**Formats that prove and build trust** — best for Problem-Aware through Most-Aware
- Testimonial / UGC
- Social Proof Montage
- Consumer Response

**Formats that drive action** — best for Product-Aware, Most-Aware
- Static
- Text Message
- Direct Response

### The format principle

Match the format's purpose to the awareness stage's goal. If the stage calls for revealing a problem, use formats that reveal. If the stage calls for proving superiority, use formats that prove.

Any format can work at any stage when the messaging is right. Format is an execution decision that builds on everything that came before it.

## The Full Map

```
Step 1: Understand the Product
    ↓ What are the features?

Step 2: Understand the Customer
    ↓ Who are they? Broad research → personas → micropersonas.

Step 3: Identify Primary Organizing Principle
    ↓ Pain-First or Desire-First?

Step 3: Map persona to pain or desire → Micropersona
    ↓ Choose the intersection. Go deep on how this person experiences this pain or desire.

Step 4: Define the Offer
    ↓ For this person, with this problem or desire — which features matter and what do they deliver?

Step 5: Choose your funnel stage
    ↓ Who are you reaching? What does your content need to accomplish?

Step 6: Choose your messaging lens
    ↓ How will you communicate the offer at this stage? What lens opens the conversation?

Step 7: Execute in formats
    ↓ What visual format is the right container for this message at this stage?
```
