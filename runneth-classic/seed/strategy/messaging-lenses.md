# The 11 Messaging Lenses

The lens is the creative approach used to convey the offer to a specific person at a specific awareness stage. Same offer, different lens, different ad. The stage you choose in `/runneth/references/creative-strategy-engine.md` naturally points to the lenses that fit.

This file bundles Alysha's eleven lenses as a closed taxonomy. Current Runneth's `creative-strategy-engine.md` reference covers messaging-angle structure but does not enumerate the lenses. This is the supplement.

## The lens defines the hook

The lens you choose becomes the hook concept. The hook is the opening expression of the offer, through this lens, for this person, at this stage. Use these lenses when generating hooks (via `hook-writing` and `hook-tactics`) or building concepts (via `creative-mechanics` and `creative-strategy-engine`).

## The 11 lenses

### 1. Desired Outcome 🌟

What's the best-case version of their life if this problem is solved? Lead with where they want to be. The transformation, not the problem.

**Best for:** Solution-Aware, Product-Aware, Most-Aware. Audiences who already know the problem exists and want to see the destination.

**Bad for:** Unaware, Problem-Aware. You can't sell a transformation before they recognize the starting state.

### 2. Pain Agitation 😣

Don't rush to the solution. Sit inside the pain with them. Describe it in detail — what it feels like, what it costs them, how it shows up in their daily life. When someone reads your hook and thinks "this person gets exactly what I'm going through," they stop.

**Best for:** Problem-Aware audiences who know the pain but haven't felt urgency to fix it. Also useful for Solution-Aware as a reminder of why this matters.

**Bad for:** Most-Aware. They've already decided; agitation feels gratuitous.

### 3. Use Case 💡

Show them a specific, recognizable moment from their real life where this product fits naturally. Not a demo — a scene. The more specific the situation, the harder it is to scroll past.

**Best for:** Solution-Aware and Product-Aware. Helps them see the product in their actual life.

**Bad for:** Unaware. They don't yet have the context to recognize the use case as relevant to them.

### 4. Consequences ⚠️

What gets worse if they don't solve this? Lead with the cost of inaction. The negative trajectory they're already on makes the solution feel urgent rather than optional.

**Best for:** Problem-Aware and Solution-Aware. Adds urgency without manufacturing it.

**Bad for:** Most-Aware. They've decided; piling on consequences is overkill.

### 5. Acceptance 🤷

What frustration or limitation have they normalized because they don't believe it can be solved — or have tried and given up on? Position the product as proof they don't have to accept this. Change is possible.

**Best for:** Problem-Aware and Solution-Aware. Works especially well for categories where past failures have created learned helplessness.

**Bad for:** Unaware (they haven't accepted anything yet) and Most-Aware (they already believe change is possible).

### 6. Education 📚

What don't they know about the problem, the solution, or the product that you can teach them? What gap can you fill to surface the problem, make it feel more urgent, or shift their perception of the solution? This isn't about busting a myth — it's about teaching something new.

**Best for:** Unaware and Problem-Aware. Education is how you create demand where none existed.

**Bad for:** Most-Aware. They don't want a lesson; they want a CTA.

### 7. Misconceptions 🤔

What common misconception is holding the customer back? What belief needs to be rebutted for them to take a next step? This isn't about educating — it's about removing a blocker between them and change.

**Best for:** Solution-Aware and Product-Aware. Specifically when objections are dominant.

**Bad for:** Unaware. They don't yet hold a misconception; they hold no opinion.

### 8. Comparison 🌱

What have they already tried that didn't work? How does this product compare? Position the product as the solution through Us vs. Them or Before and After framing.

**Best for:** Solution-Aware and Product-Aware. The active comparison stage.

**Bad for:** Unaware, Problem-Aware. Nothing to compare against yet.

### 9. Features and Benefits ⚙

Which specific aspect of the product, when explained properly, makes the customer realize this is the right solution? Lead with the feature that most directly addresses their pain, then name the benefit it delivers.

**Best for:** Product-Aware and Most-Aware. Audiences who need product-specific reasons to choose this over alternatives.

**Bad for:** Unaware, Problem-Aware. Feature-led hooks rarely earn attention from people who haven't recognized the problem yet.

### 10. Objections 🛑

What specific doubt or hesitation would stop them from buying right now? Address it head-on. Turning skepticism into the hook itself disarms resistance before it forms.

**Best for:** Product-Aware and Most-Aware. The stages where objections are top of mind.

**Bad for:** Unaware and Problem-Aware. They don't have objections yet because they haven't reached consideration.

### 11. Identity 🧍

What insecurity do they carry about who they are today as it relates to this category? Who do they look at and think "I want to be like that"? Position the product as the path to becoming that person.

**Best for:** Any stage when identity is the driver (luxury, aspiration, status, lifestyle categories). Particularly powerful at Problem-Aware and Solution-Aware for identity-led purchases.

**Bad for:** Pure functional categories where identity isn't part of the purchase logic.

## How to use this with hooks

When generating hooks via `hook-writing` and `hook-tactics`:

1. Take the messaging angle from the `creative-strategy-engine` (pain × persona intersection).
2. Pick the lens that fits the awareness stage. Use the "best for" notes above to filter.
3. The lens defines what the hook is doing emotionally. The hook tactic from `hook-tactics` defines the structural frame (Question, Confession, Bold Claim, etc.). They compose.
4. Write the verbatim hook so it expresses the lens through the tactic.

A Pain Agitation lens through a Confession tactic reads differently than a Pain Agitation lens through a Bold Claim tactic. Both can be valid for the same messaging angle.

## Anti-pattern

Defaulting to Features and Benefits for every brief. Most failures here are mismatched stage and lens — running Feature-led hooks at Problem-Aware (no one cares yet) or Comparison hooks at Unaware (nothing to compare against).
